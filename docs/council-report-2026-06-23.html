✓ Where the Council Agrees

The data foundation is broken before any metric runs. reference_context_ids is empty across all rows, so IDContextRecall — the only metric grounded in actual document identity — produces no output. The metric exists, the infrastructure runs it, and it silently contributes nothing. You're evaluating retrieval using keyword substring matching against the same documents you're retrieving from. The relevance signal is circular.

20 questions is a smoke test, not an eval pipeline. The surface_conflict class has one example. refuse_if_not_found is similarly thin. ~90% of the dataset tests the happy path (cite_answer), repeatedly.

reference_answer exists for every row but the metrics that consume it are off by default. factual_correctness and context_recall both require it. Ground truth you aren't using.

Async-to-sync bridging is a latent reliability risk. Serial execution with sync wrappers over async Ragas scorers will produce intermittent, hard-to-diagnose hangs at scale — not clean errors.

⚡ Where the Council Clashes

Scale now vs. fix foundation first. The Expansionist argues for 500+ LLM-generated questions immediately. The Contrarian, Executor, and Outsider all argue the opposite: scaling bad labels produces more noise, not more signal. The Executor is explicit — don't expand until reference_context_ids have real ground-truth file_id values.

The Expansionist is strategically correct (more coverage would help) but tactically reckless. LLM-generated QA pairs from Qwen inherit whatever distributional biases and hallucinations the generator has. If you generate 500 questions before fixing the labeling substrate, you've scaled the problem. Fix substrate first, then scale.

Whether behavioral categories are worth expanding. The Expansionist wants to add synthesize_across_docs, defer_to_authority, acknowledge_ambiguity. The Contrarian notes that surface_conflict — already in the schema — has no scoring mechanism. Adding more behavioral classes before you can measure the ones you have is overhead, not evaluation signal.

🔍 Blind Spots the Council Caught 5/5 reviewers

Russian-language LLM judge calibration — the most important finding from peer review, unanimously.

Ragas Faithfulness and AnswerRelevancy were designed and validated on English text. The underlying embeddings and entailment logic have not been benchmarked on Russian bureaucratic register — the specific register of warehouse regulations, job descriptions, and HR policy documents.

A hallucinated answer about warehouse safety procedures written in fluent Russian bureaucratic prose could score high on Faithfulness because the judge detects stylistic coherence, not factual accuracy. In a domain where wrong answers about safety regulations have real consequences, a mis-calibrated judge is not just a statistical problem — it's a liability.

Nobody asked: Are your Ragas prompt templates in Russian? Does Qwen/DashScope reliably detect entailment in this specific register? Have you spot-checked scores against human judgment?

📋 The Recommendation

  1. Verify Russian-language judge calibration before trusting any metric. Take 20 scored outputs — 10 good answers, 10 intentionally degraded ones — and compare Ragas scores to human judgment from a native Russian speaker familiar with HR documents. If Faithfulness and AnswerRelevancy don't correlate with human judgment at r > 0.7, your metrics are decorative. This can be done in a day and gates everything else.
  2. Fill reference_context_ids for all existing questions and enable factual_correctness by default. 2–3 hours of labeling work. Activates IDContextRecall (your only retrieval metric grounded in real document identity) and turns on factual correctness scoring against ground truth you already have. These two changes give more real signal than any new metric.
  3. Add 5+ questions per underrepresented behavior class (refuse_if_not_found, surface_conflict) before scaling. One surface_conflict example is not a test; it's an anecdote. Also: consolidate context_recall/factual_correctness into the Phoenix evaluator path — the two-script split is artificial friction.

Do not scale to 200+ questions until steps 1 and 2 are complete. Scaling before that multiplies noise.

⚡ The One Thing to Do First

Spot-check Russian-language LLM judge calibration: take 10 clearly correct and 10 intentionally wrong RAG outputs on your existing questions, score them with Ragas, and compare against human judgment. If the scores don't reliably separate good from bad answers in Russian, every other improvement you make will be measuring the wrong thing.
Full advisor responses
The Contrarian — "The relevance signal is fake"
The Contrarian

The evals pipeline has a critical flaw that undermines everything else: the relevance signal is fake.

Binary hit@5 based on keyword substring matching in document text is not relevance — it's a string search dressed up as an evaluation metric. You're computing NDCG over labels that have no actual grounding in whether a document is useful for answering the question. When your precision@5 is 0.4, you don't know if retrieval is bad or if your substring hints are too broad/narrow.

20 questions is not an eval dataset — it's a smoke test. A single bad question can swing Faithfulness by 5%. Ragas best practices assume hundreds of samples minimum.

surface_conflict is evaluated how, exactly? There's no LLM judge or rubric for detecting when the system correctly identifies conflicting documents vs. hallucinating a conflict.

The async-to-sync bridge for Ragas scorers is a red flag. Intermittent hangs appear as timeouts, not errors. Failure modes here are invisible.

Fix data quality and label validity before adding more metrics — more metrics on bad labels is more noise, not more signal.

First Principles Thinker — "You're measuring proxies of proxies"
First Principles Thinker

The question assumes the current eval structure is the right structure. It isn't.

What problem are we actually solving? "Does this RAG system give HR staff correct, trustworthy answers about warehouse regulations?" The current pipeline measures proxies of proxies. Ragas Faithfulness measures whether the answer is grounded in retrieved context — but if retrieval is broken, Faithfulness scores high on wrong documents. You're measuring coherence within a mistake.

The expected_behavior taxonomy is the most valuable signal you have — and it's the least developed. You have ~20 questions total. That's not an eval, that's a smoke test.

The actual gap: you don't know whether real users are getting real answers to real questions. You have no production query distribution. Mine production logs for actual user queries — that's your eval distribution. Design your eval around the failure mode that causes the most harm in this domain: hallucinated safety regulations? Missing return procedures?

The Expansionist — "Scale the dataset aggressively"
The Expansionist

The evals pipeline is thinking too small. Twenty questions is a prototype, not a pipeline.

Scale the dataset aggressively. With LLM-assisted generation (use the Qwen endpoint you already have), you can generate 500+ QA pairs from your actual documents in an afternoon. Cover edge cases: cross-document synthesis, adversarial paraphrases, multi-hop reasoning questions.

Treat the eval pipeline as a regression harness, not a one-shot run. Wire evals to every retrieval config change. Track NDCG@5 trend lines across chunking/model experiments in Phoenix.

Your expected_behavior taxonomy is a product differentiator. Expand it — add synthesize_across_docs, defer_to_authority, acknowledge_ambiguity. This turns your evals from a quality check into a behavioral specification for the entire system.

Note from peer review: Strategically correct, tactically reckless. Fix the substrate before scaling.

The Outsider — "You're running dead metrics"
The Outsider

~27 of your ~30 questions are cite_answer. 90% of the dataset tests the same thing. You have 1 surface_conflict example. One.

reference_context_ids exists in the schema but is populated for zero questions. IDContextRecall silently skips every single row. You are running infrastructure for a metric that produces no results.

The expected_source_hints check does substring matching. A document about "возврат товаров" could satisfy a hint looking for "возврат брака" if those words appear anywhere in the document. You're measuring string co-occurrence, not retrieval quality.

reference_answer is populated for all rows, but context_recall and factual_correctness are optional and off by default. You have ground truth you aren't using.

Three fixes: (1) fill reference_context_ids for cite_answer questions; (2) add 5+ refuse_if_not_found and surface_conflict cases; (3) make factual_correctness default-on.

The Executor — "Three concrete actions, in order"
The Executor

1. Parallelize the eval loop — do it Monday. At ~3-5s per question + LLM scoring, serial execution is already slow and will become a hard blocker at 200 questions. Use asyncio.gather or ThreadPoolExecutor. Phoenix's run_experiment accepts an async task function — use it.

2. Add reference_context_ids to every question — 2-3 hours of labeling. Run uv run scripts/query.py on each question, check which documents came back, confirm the right ones, write the file_ids. This activates IDContextRecall and gives you real retrieval quality signal.

3. Consolidate the two-script split. _phoenix_evaluators hard-errors on context_recall/factual_correctness, forcing a separate script. reference_answer is already passed through _to_phoenix_dataset_rows. Add context_recall as a Phoenix evaluator directly. Remove the artificial friction.

What not to do yet: Don't expand to 200+ questions before retrieval scoring is based on real file_id ground truth.
